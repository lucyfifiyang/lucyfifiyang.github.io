My Story: COVID-19 Testing

March 2020: a world shutting down. An email from UW Medicine pleaded my department for volunteers to help conduct COVID-19 tests due to unprecedented demand. Despite just completing the bioengineering PhD qualification exam, I could not sit tight and take the customary short post-exam break. Instead, I joined UW Medicine in the fight against COVID-19. The night after my exam, I biked to UW Medical Center to work under the Specimen Processing Services (SPS) and prepare COVID-19 samples for testing. Foege may be closed, but I returned to work! I worked alongside regular employees and travel nurses, sorting and labeling hundreds of specimen samples each night. Quickly mastering the processing steps for COVID-19 PCR tests, I also learned how to process specimen for more routine lab work and how to perform extra tasks, such as sending and receiving samples through the pneumatic tube system. Most importantly, I learned new things that I could take to my research. Tests were slow to process, sometimes taking a week to return results to patients. UW SPS and Virology were nearly at their maximum processing capabilities given that the PCR machines were back-ordered and even basic supplies were scarce. When my professor asked for a volunteer to help investigate DNA aptamers for binding SARS-CoV-2, I quickly offered my assistance even though I had not researched aptamers before. I saw an opportunity to apply aptamers to COVID-19 testing: if we developed aptamers for rapid lateral flow tests, the diagnosis would be ready in the matter of minutes unlike the PCR tests I handled. Although I spent time away from research, I laid the foundation to my PhD thesis work.

My Story: COVID-19 Research



COVID-19 patient samples on dry ice await processing.

Our lab investigates aptamers and applies them a range of fields, including cancer therapy and now COVID-19 diagnostics. Aptamers are short single-stranded DNA that can bind and recognize other molecules, such as cells and proteins. The aptamer's ability to bind is simialr to protein antibodies, but aptamers are cheaper to manufacture and can withstand larger ranges in temperature, buffer, and other conditions. We were interested in discovering aptamers that can bind the spike protein of SARS-CoV-2, which is the virus that causes COVID-19.
