My Story: COVID-19 Testing

March 2020: a world shutting down. An email from UW Medicine pleaded for volunteers to help conduct COVID-19 tests due to unprecedented demand. Despite just completing the bioengineering PhD qualification exam, I could not sit tight and take the customary short post-exam break. I was eager to tackle my next step. So, I joined UW Medicine in the fight against COVID-19. The night after my exam, I biked to UW Medical Center to work under the Specimen Processing Services (SPS) and prepare COVID-19 samples for testing. Foege may be closed, but I returned to work! I worked alongside hospital lab techs and travel nurses, sorting and labeling hundreds of specimen samples each night. Most importantly, I gained first-hand experience of COVID-19 testing that enabled me to identify problems, such as slow test turnaround times. I saw an opportunity to apply aptamers to COVID-19 testing: if we developed aptamers for rapid lateral flow tests, the diagnosis could be ready in a matter of minutes, much faster than the PCR tests I handled. Although I spent time away from research, I gained invaluable perception into COVID-19 testing and this laid the foundation for my PhD thesis work in the Pun Lab.

My Story: COVID-19 Research



Analyzing lateral flow test strips with Joey.

At the Pun Lab, I lead the investigation on SARS-CoV-2-binding aptamers (short single-stranded DNA that can bind proteins) for rapid tests, such as lateral flow test strips and electrochemical detection. Despite having little background in aptamers when I first started, I not only quickly mastered our lab's aptamer protocols but also collaborated with other UW research groups. We obtained valuable patient samples from the Seattle Flu Study team and I learned new techniques from other UW Bioengineering labs, including lateral flow test strip manufacturing. Last summer, I was a first author on our publication about SARS-CoV-2 aptamers in Angewandte Chemie and presented my work at the 2021 Oligonucleotide Therapeutics Conference as a competitively-selected early-career speaker. Very recently, I first-authored a manuscript under review at Analytical Chemistry on aptamer-based SARS-CoV-2 lateral flow tests, which is the rapid test I envisioned at SPS. Thanks to the unique resources available at UW, I've been able to collaborate with talented scientists on problems I witnessed firsthand, bolstering the research I can share with the scientific community.

During this project, I also mentored UW bioengineering junior Joey to support my project and further his own research career. After I taught him key protocols, Joey was quickly able to conduct independent research and perform crucial experiments for discovering new SARS-CoV-2-binding aptamers. Through my mentorship, Joey was able to greatly improve his confidence as a scientist. As a result, Joey received the Mary Gates Research Scholarship this past quarter, and I'm proud to continue to cultivate his scientific abilities for months to come.

My Story: Laser-cutting

Just as I mentored Joey at the bench, I also trained two UW design majors as my online store assistants. Camille is a junior in visual design and Robin (my little brother) is a senior in industrial design. Since starting at UW, I've maintained my small business for selling laser-cut items, such as keychains, coasters, game token sets, display cases, costume props, and more. As orders grew to the tune of hundreds per month, I hired and taught my assistants how to prepare products and ship orders while also managing everyone's schedules to meet client deadlines. I gained valuable experience in how to manage trainees, which came in handy when I started to mentor Joey in the lab. Most importantly, I connect the dots and apply laser-cutting to nearly all facets of my life experiences. I not only craft functional pieces like "ear savers" for SPS co-workers, but also great presents like biologically-accurate cell coasters to show gratitude to research collaborators.

My Story: Pokémon

Beyond campus, I'm a keystone in the competitive Pokémon community of UW and Seattle. As a member of the Pokémon Professor program, I organize and host casual open play (called "Pokémon League") and competitive tournaments for both the trading card game and the video game. When I first arrived on campus, I saw an opportunity to ignite the local Pokémon scene at the UW. Noticing a dearth of Leagues in Seattle, I started a weekly League in the U District's The Dreaming Comics and Games, and also hosted weekly League at MOX Boarding House in Ballard. As Leagues are open to the public, I warmly welcome players of all ages, orientations, and abilities to play. Sometimes it is challenging to teach children who are learning to read with Pokémon cards, or manage the needs of guests on the spectrum. These experiences have bolstered my patience and ability to serve others even when situations leave my comfort zone. Today, I am still the only Seattle organizer to hold officially sanctioned video game tournaments, bringing in UW students, Portlanders, and even Canadians together for weekend Pokémon battles.
