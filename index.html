My Story: COVID-19 Testing

March 2020: a world shutting down. An email from UW Medicine pleaded my department for volunteers to help conduct COVID-19 tests due to unprecedented demand. Despite just completing the bioengineering PhD qualification exam, I could not sit tight and take the customary short post-exam break. I was ready to tackle my next step. So, I joined UW Medicine in the fight against COVID-19. The night after my exam, I biked to UW Medical Center to work under the Specimen Processing Services (SPS) and prepare COVID-19 samples for testing. Foege may be closed, but I returned to work! I worked alongside regular employees and travel nurses, sorting and labeling hundreds of specimen samples each night. Quickly mastering the processing steps for COVID-19 PCR tests, I also learned how to process specimen for more routine lab work and how to perform extra tasks, such as sending and receiving samples through the pneumatic tube system. Most importantly, I learned new things that I could take to my research. Tests were slow to process, sometimes taking a week to return results to patients. UW SPS and Virology were nearly at their maximum processing capabilities given that the PCR machines were back-ordered and even basic supplies were scarce. When my professor asked for a volunteer to help investigate DNA aptamers for binding SARS-CoV-2, I quickly offered my assistance even though I had not researched aptamers before. I saw an opportunity to apply aptamers to COVID-19 testing: if we developed aptamers for rapid lateral flow tests, the diagnosis would be ready in the matter of minutes unlike the PCR tests I handled. Although I spent time away from research, I gained invaluable perception into COVID-19 testing and this laid the foundation to my PhD thesis work.

My Story: COVID-19 Research



Applying AptaFlow to a test sample.

Our lab investigates aptamers and applies them a range of fields, including cancer therapy and now COVID-19 diagnostics. Aptamers are short single-stranded DNA that can bind and recognize other molecules, such as cells and proteins. The aptamer's ability to bind is similar to protein antibodies, but aptamers are cheaper to manufacture and can withstand larger ranges in temperature, buffer, and other conditions. These are key features that can make an aptamer-based lateral flow test cheaper and more convenient to use! We were interested in discovering aptamers that can bind the spike protein of SARS-CoV-2, which is the virus that causes COVID-19. Because SARS-CoV-2 research was very new at the time, we encountered many unexpected challenges while conducting the investigation, not to mention that I was new to the world of aptamers. For example, I encountered issues in the aptamer discovery process and nuances in the spike protein's structure. Despite this, I helped bring our research to fruition: Last summer, I co-authored our first publication on SARS-CoV-2 aptamers and presented my work at the 2021 Oligonucleotide Therapeutics Conference, which selected only five young scientist presenters in a competitive application process. Thanks to my adaptability, I've been able to find opportunities within obstacles and elevate my research further. Right now, I'm applying aptamers to lateral flow- and electrochemical-based detection of SARS-CoV-2. My Story: Laser-cutting

My Story: Pokémon

For the UW community, Seattle community, and beyond, I organize and staff official Pokémon events for the trading card game and video game. Local events at places like MOX Boarding House are hosted by members of the Pokémon Professor program, which background checks and certifies volunteers to organize and judge events. I organize and host casual open play and competitive tournaments for both the trading card game and the video game. When I first moved to Seattle at the beginning of my PhD, I saw an opportunity to kindle the local Pokémon scene at the UW. There was no local host for the video game, and only two local game stores in Seattle that hosted card game events. So, I hosted weekly open-play events in the U District's The Dreaming Comics and Games (now closed), and also at MOX Boarding House Seattle. I organized the only officially sanctioned video game tournaments in Seattle, bringing in UW students, Portland residents, and even Canadians together for Pokémon battles. Thanks to my ample local experience, I applied and was accepted as a staff member for regional large tournaments in Portland, Anaheim, Dallas, and other major cities. Attracting hundreds of Pokémon players across the country vying for cash prizes, these tournaments also brought in seasoned Pokémon Professors as judges. I not only learned useful teamwork skills, but also occasionally was chosen to lead judge teams. ______ Although in-person event were and remain canceled due to COVID-19, I persisted in contributing to the community through virtual means. Due to my previous experience hosting Seattle events and volunteering at large tournaments, I was chosen to judge and head judge for the only official online Pokémon circuit.
